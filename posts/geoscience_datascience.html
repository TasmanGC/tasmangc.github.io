Exploration Geoscience & Data Science

Tasman Gillfeather-Clark · 2023 · 7 min read

Exploration Geoscience

In the exploration geoscience field, considerable effort is expended by field technicians, crew leaders, and geoscientists — to acquire, process, and interpret the signatures and traces of geological reality that lie just below the surface.

Glimpses of reality; captured in assays, geophysical logs, and stereo nets.

The reason we brave harsh conditions, equipment failures, and the miasma of field life is because, at our core, exploration geoscientists are addicted to discovery. The idea of being the boots on the ground when the next Olympic Dam is pegged, or the first mine to successfully operate under deep cover — such aspirations drive many a project geo to late nights going over the data just one more time.

Our biggest fear by extension is missing something. Such ideals are well characterised by author Malcolm Knox, where he refers to many early exploration personalities of Australia as "nearly men" — forgotten to the passages of time, they drilled one too few holes, stopped the survey 1km short.

I'm proud to say our industry has diversified much in the past few decades — no longer just "nearly men" dominate the landscape. But something that hasn't changed: no matter who you are, if you work in exploration, you don't want to miss anything.

Knowledge Discovery

The process of knowledge discovery and generating insight leads to sleepless nights poring over tables and old reports, looking for something your predecessors missed. Asking yourself the age-old question: what am I missing?

Sadly the modern geoscientist is torn between the floods of new data coming in and the legacy dataset they've been meaning to get around to. As skillsets grow to accommodate this flood of data — with the average geo knowing their way around an Excel macro, a taste for Python, or an R script — these efforts, while valuable, are rarely scalable.

The scale of the data, the training of ML models, the validation and maintenance work required to keep it running — this is out of scope for the 0.2 FTE a supportive manager is likely to give them. The other 0.8 FTE spent on BAU will always be the priority. So sadly many of these projects will die on the laptop desktops of passionate people who had to prepare for the next monthly handover presentation.

So what's the answer? Hand the project over to the Data Science team and hope for the best? Adamantly no. The integration of geoscience knowledge in the process of scaling and deploying these projects is crucial. The process of acceptance testing, data review, and model refinement should always integrate domain knowledge. Otherwise the little voice in the geo's mind will never go away: what are they missing?

If those with sign-off doubt the veracity of the DS team, you end up with a stalemate — the geo refuses to give insight until they believe the DS team will listen, while the DS team starts to view their SME as an unreliable source. Money lost, time lost, discoveries lost.

The Role of Data Science in Exploration

So we've been introduced to the mindset of the average exploration geoscientist. But what of the humble data scientist — what is it they want? Startlingly, they want the exact same thing: they want to find something. A trend, a pattern, a signal in the noise. The only difference is that they care little for its geological significance. A trend is a trend.

These sentiments will ring true to any geo who works with DS on a regular basis. The DS team will raise all the patterns they find — that's arguably their job. Sometimes the cause will be so gut-wrenchingly obvious to the geo that they'll dismiss the idea of DS altogether. But sometimes they raise something discovered by chance that lights a spark in an exploration manager and prompts three more holes "just in case."

Data scientists are a wealth of statistical techniques, machine learning expertise, and scalable programming. The question is how to connect that capability meaningfully to the domain knowledge that gives it direction. That connection — built through trust, shared language, and genuine curiosity about each other's craft — is the real work.
