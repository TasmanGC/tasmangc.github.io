Self-Organising Maps for Geoscience

Tasman Gillfeather-Clark · 2021 · 6 min read
← All posts

What is a Self-Organising Map?

A self-organising map (SOM) is an unsupervised neural network that produces a low-dimensional — typically two-dimensional — representation of a high-dimensional dataset. Unlike most neural networks, SOMs are trained using competitive learning rather than error-correction: neurons compete to respond to a given input, and the winner adjusts both itself and its neighbours to become more like that input.

The result is a map where similar inputs are placed near each other. Topology is preserved: things that are close in the original high-dimensional space remain close on the map. This makes SOMs particularly useful in geoscience, where you often have many co-located measurements — geochemistry, geophysics, remote sensing — and you want to understand how the landscape clusters without specifying the clusters in advance.

The Broken Hill Case Study

The application here uses multi-source geophysical data over the Broken Hill region of New South Wales — a heavily mineralised district with complex stratigraphy and a long history of exploration. The dataset included airborne radiometrics (K, Th, U), magnetics, and a digital terrain model.

The goal was land classification: can we use the SOM to identify coherent geological units and surface expressions from the combined geophysical signature alone, without feeding in any known geology as supervision?

Running the SOM without the DTM produced cleaner geological separations than the full dataset. The terrain model, it turned out, was introducing a strong topographic signal that masked subtler geophysical contrasts. The image at the top of this post shows the SOM output without DTM — each colour represents a node on the trained map, and spatially coherent regions correspond to consistent geophysical character.

What the Results Actually Mean

The most important thing about a SOM result in a geoscience context is that it is a hypothesis, not a classification. The clusters have no inherent labels. Interpreting them requires domain knowledge: you need to look at what you know about the geology, compare the SOM nodes to known lithologies, and build your interpretation from there.

In the Broken Hill study, several nodes correlated strongly with known outcropping lithologies — metasediments, felsic volcanics, and the BIF horizon. Others were ambiguous, representing either mixed pixels or units without strong geophysical contrast. The ambiguity itself is informative: it tells you where you need more data, or where your current sensor suite can't discriminate.

This is the right way to use a SOM in exploration: not as a black box that produces a geological map, but as a tool for structuring the conversation between the data and the geologist. The algorithm does the hard work of finding the structure; the geologist does the hard work of deciding what that structure means.

Limitations and Next Steps

SOMs are sensitive to the choice of training parameters — map size, learning rate, neighbourhood function — and to the normalisation of the input data. A SOM trained on un-normalised data will be dominated by the features with the largest dynamic range, which may not be the geologically meaningful ones.

They also don't handle missing data gracefully. In a regional geophysical dataset, coverage gaps are common, and the SOM will either ignore those pixels or produce artefacts at the boundaries.

The natural extension of this work is to run the SOM iteratively — using the initial classification to guide targeted follow-up data acquisition, then retraining on the improved dataset. At each step the map gets better, and the geologist's understanding of what they're looking at deepens. That loop — data, model, interpretation, data — is the real engine of knowledge discovery in exploration.
