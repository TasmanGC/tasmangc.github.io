What Field Work Teaches You That No Dataset Can

Tasman Gillfeather-Clark · May 2026 · 6 min read
← All posts

Reading the Rock

There is a moment that every field geologist knows. You're standing at an outcrop that should be straightforward — a simple contact, maybe, or a fold axis you've been chasing for two days. And the rock just doesn't cooperate. The foliation is inconsistent, the contact is gradational where it should be sharp, and that xenolith you're staring at shouldn't be there at all.

What you do next is the thing no dataset can teach you.

You don't go back to the model. You don't average it away or flag it as an outlier. You sit with it. You try to understand what process could have produced what you're seeing, even if that process isn't in your hypothesis. You update your mental model of the whole area based on one hand-specimen sized piece of evidence.

This is pattern recognition at its hardest — not recognising a pattern you've been trained on, but recognising that the pattern you expected is absent, and that this absence is meaningful.

The Measurement Problem

Field data is almost never what you actually want to measure. You want to know the bulk composition of a rock unit, so you take a chip sample from the surface. You want to know the subsurface geometry, so you measure strike and dip at outcrop. You want to know the extent of mineralisation, so you drill a hole every 200 metres and hope the spacing makes sense.

Every piece of data you collect is a proxy for something else. And the distance between your proxy and your target — that's where all the interesting error lives.

This is something that takes years to internalise, and it fundamentally changes how you approach a dataset. When a geologist looks at a geochemical assay, they're not just reading a number. They're asking: what was the sample preparation method? Was this drilled in the wet season? Is there a reason the collar coordinates look slightly off for this hole? The number is almost secondary.

Data scientists tend to learn this lesson the hard way — after a model performs beautifully in validation and then falls apart in production because the training data had a subtle collection bias no one thought to document. Field geoscientists learn it on day one.

Uncertainty as a Feature

Ask a geologist how confident they are in an interpretation and they'll give you a range. They'll tell you what they can say with high confidence, what they're inferring, and what's frankly a guess based on regional analogy. They'll mark their cross-sections with solid lines for known contacts and dashed lines for inferred ones. The uncertainty is part of the deliverable.

Machine learning models tend to produce point estimates. The output of a classification model is a class label; the output of a regression is a number. You have to work to get uncertainty out of most standard approaches — calibration, ensembles, conformal prediction. It's almost treated as an afterthought.

The geological mindset flips this. Uncertainty isn't a problem to be minimised and then reported; it's a first-class part of the result. A model that says "I'm 90% confident" is telling you something important. A model that says "confident" without qualification is hiding something.

Spending time in the field teaches you to be suspicious of confident answers to hard questions. That suspicion is exactly what you want when you're evaluating a model trained on imperfect data.

So What?

I'm not making an argument that data scientists should all go spend a season in the Pilbara (though I would personally recommend it). I'm making a narrower point: that the habits of mind you build through field work — sitting with ambiguity, interrogating the gap between measurement and meaning, treating uncertainty as information — are exactly the habits that make someone good at working with real data in real systems.

The best data scientists I've worked with tend to have this disposition regardless of background. They're the ones who ask "why does this column have 40% nulls?" before they impute them. Who wonder whether the train-test split is temporally consistent. Who notice that the validation metric looks too good and go looking for the leak.

Field work just accelerates the development of that disposition, because it forces you to confront these questions in a context where you can't hide behind the abstraction of a spreadsheet. The rock is in front of you. It is what it is. Go figure it out.
