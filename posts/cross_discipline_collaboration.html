Cross-Disciplinary Team Dynamics

Tasman Gillfeather-Clark · 2022 · 8 min read
← All posts

These reflections are meant to capture the lessons I learned during my various collaborations with industry, government agencies, and research bodies — as part of the projects I completed during my years attending Macquarie University and the University of Western Australia.

Keeping Everyone Engaged

Arguably the most challenging part of any project is keeping everyone engaged. With some estimates putting only 13% of people engaged with a given project, it's unrealistic to expect members of our teams to work at 100% all the time. So what strategies can we employ to create an environment that makes it easier for all members of the team to work together?

The Harvard Business Review has some good articles and discourse on the subject, but I'll limit my thoughts here based on these ideas and my own experiences working with teams in geoscience and software development. Getting the obvious out of the way — running a successful team or project is about: communication, collaboration, and consensus derived compromise.

Communication — We have all worked on projects or in organisations where no-one talks to each other. Silos, or isolated teams, arise from a breakdown in communication and ultimately everyone suffers. Either efforts are duplicated, information is withheld, or people just stop trying. Rarely will these silos arise from malice — instead just poor communication, poor understanding of the vision or goal of the team. Sprinkle in enough time and you'll see walls start to grow.

These boundaries established by poor communication can thankfully be broken down with good communication. Building bridges, collaborative meetings, and prioritising helping other teams cuts through these silos often quicker than they can be established.

Collaboration — No team should feel like a one-person act. Most issues with collaboration don't arise as a matter of hard worker vs lazy worker — everyone works differently and collaboration is based on respect. Look around your team and see the members as individuals, not just skillsets. Their way of working may inform the tasks they're suited to as much as their training.

Consensus Derived Compromise — a vision your team doesn't agree on is one you will never reach. A good team trusts each other and recognises the expertise of their colleagues. A great team understands the vision of the project and is willing to sacrifice part of their individual values to achieve it. In the teams I worked with for the NGEA and FAA, what drove our success wasn't an individual's vision — it was a blending of ideas, distilled into a clear goal, which we all worked towards.

The Usual Suspects

Let's start by broadly categorising the types of skillsets we encounter in geoscience and software development:

  • Data Scientists — great statistical tools, love a challenge, obsessed with pattern recognition. They typically lack domain expertise, focusing on values rather than their meaning. They may not focus on aspects of code quality like testing.
  • Geoscientists — deep understanding of geological meaning and how problems manifest in their domain. Can only hold so much data in their head, and are time-poor due to BAU work. Often hesitant to share domain knowledge due to high penalties associated with getting it wrong.
  • Software Engineers — their code is an art form; their systems run without downtime. Great understanding of network security, deployment, and release cycles. Their priorities can cause conflicts with contributors who don't understand the importance of the processes they implement.

Knowledge, Diversity and Collaboration

The intersection of these three disciplines is where the most interesting work happens — and also where the most friction lives. The goal is not to turn geoscientists into software engineers, or data scientists into geologists. It's to build enough shared language that each group can ask good questions of the others.

Diversity of thought within a project team is genuinely protective. Homogenous teams tend to share blind spots. When a geoscientist, a data scientist, and a software engineer are all looking at the same problem, they will see different risks, ask different questions, and propose different solutions. The friction that generates is not a cost — it's the whole point.

The Importance of a Clear Vision

All of the above falls apart without a clear, shared vision. A team can have excellent communication, deep mutual respect, and diverse expertise — and still fail to deliver, if no-one can articulate what success looks like.

The role of the team lead is crucial here. Not to dictate the vision, but to hold it: to keep translating it back into terms each sub-group can act on, to notice when day-to-day pressures are pulling people off course, and to create the conditions where the team can re-orient when they drift.

In my experience, the clearest sign a team has found its footing is when members from different disciplines start spontaneously explaining each other's work — when the geo can articulate why the deploy pipeline matters, or the software engineer can explain why the sample spacing matters. That mutual literacy is the thing worth building.
